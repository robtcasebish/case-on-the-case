On Christmas Eve, 1945, a fire broke out at the Fayetteville, West Virginia home
of George and Jennie Sodder. The parents and four of their ten children escaped.
**Five children did not.** When the smoke cleared, the family expected to find
the worst. Instead, they found something stranger: nothing at all.

## A fire with no remains

The house burned to its foundation in roughly 45 minutes. When the family later
searched the ashes, no skeletal remains of the five missing children were
recovered. A subsequent excavation of the site likewise turned up no conclusive
evidence that the children had died in the blaze.

The parents pointed to a series of unsettling details:

- The home's telephone line had been **cut**, not burned.
- A ladder normally kept against the house was found thrown some distance away.
- George Sodder's truck would not start that night, despite working earlier.
- A passing motorist later claimed to have seen children in a passing car during
  the fire.

## Decades of searching

George and Jennie Sodder refused to accept the official conclusion that the
children had perished. They erected a **billboard** along Route 16 displaying the
children's photographs and offering a reward for information. Over the years they
chased reported sightings across the country and even hired private
investigators.

In the late 1960s, Jennie received an envelope postmarked from Kentucky
containing a photograph of a young man who, the family believed, resembled one of
the missing boys grown older. The lead, like every other, went nowhere
verifiable.

## An open question

No definitive evidence has ever confirmed what happened to the five Sodder
children. The case remains a fixture of American cold-case lore precisely because
it offers two equally unsettling possibilities — a tragedy with no remains, or a
disappearance with no answer.
