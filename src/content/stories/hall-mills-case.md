In September 1922, the bodies of an Episcopal clergyman and a member of his
church choir were discovered beneath a crab apple tree in Somerset County, New
Jersey. Between them lay torn-up love letters. The case that followed became one
of the first true **media spectacles** of the American century.

## The discovery

The two victims — the Reverend Edward Hall and Eleanor Mills — were found
arranged side by side, their personal correspondence scattered around them. The
staging of the scene suggested the killings were personal, not random. Both
victims were married to other people, and rumors of an affair had already
circulated in the community.

## A trial as entertainment

The case drew enormous national press attention. When it finally reached trial
in 1926 — years after the killings — newspapers dispatched reporters by the
dozen. Telegraph wires were strung directly into the courthouse. Coverage was so
breathless that the proceedings have since been studied as an early example of
how mass media can turn a criminal trial into public theater.

A key witness, memorably nicknamed by the press, testified to having seen figures
near the scene. The defense attacked her credibility relentlessly.

## Acquittal — and silence

The jury acquitted the defendants. No one else was ever charged. The Hall–Mills
murders remain officially unsolved.

What the case left behind was less a solution than a template: the celebrity
trial, the witness-as-character, the headline that outruns the evidence. A
century later, those dynamics feel strikingly modern.
