## Who Was Involved

Jonelle Renee Matthews was twelve years old, a seventh grader who sang in the honor choir at Franklin Middle School in Greeley, Colorado. On the third week of December 1984, she was a child looking toward Christmas, the kind of ordinary anticipation that makes what followed so difficult to hold. Her family lived in a ranch-style house on the northern edge of the city, about fifty miles up the plains from Denver. When she did not come home, her parents and her older sister carried the weight of her absence for the better part of four decades, never knowing where she was or what had happened to her.

The man eventually convicted in her death was, at the time she vanished, a neighbor. Steve Pankey lived near the Matthews family in 1984. He would later move away, remarry, and reinvent himself as a perennial long-shot candidate for public office in Idaho, running for lieutenant governor and twice for governor. For most of the intervening years he was not a name the public associated with Jonelle at all. He drew the attention of investigators only decades later, after he repeatedly volunteered that he had knowledge of the crime and, at one point, sought immunity in exchange for what he claimed to know.

## What Happened

On the evening of December 20, 1984, Jonelle performed in a Christmas choir concert. Afterward, a friend's father drove her home and watched her go inside. It was around eight o'clock. The house was empty. Her own father arrived a short time later, within roughly an hour, and found no sign of her. Accounts from the period describe small, domestic details left behind: her shoes near a chair, her stockings on the couch, the ordinary debris of a child who had just walked in the door. But Jonelle was gone.

There was no ransom, no clear sign of struggle that resolved the question of what had occurred, and no body. For nearly thirty-five years that absence defined the case. Jonelle became one of the missing children of the 1980s whose faces appeared on milk cartons across the country. Her disappearance reached the highest levels of national attention: President Ronald Reagan referenced her case as his administration pressed a campaign to find missing children, and her name became attached, briefly and painfully, to a national reckoning with how easily a child could simply disappear.

## The Investigation

For decades the investigation had little to work with. There was no scene of a killing, no remains, and no confession that detectives could anchor a case to. Greeley police and, over time, the FBI kept the file open, but leads went cold, and the years accumulated. The people who loved Jonelle aged without answers.

What gradually moved to the center of the inquiry was the conduct of the man who would later stand trial. According to the prosecution's account at trial, Steve Pankey inserted himself into the case repeatedly over the years, offering statements that investigators came to view as inconsistent and, in their reading, incriminating. He was said to have referenced details that had not been made public. Yet the case that detectives were assembling was almost entirely circumstantial. There was no physical evidence directly tying Pankey to Jonelle, a limitation prosecutors themselves acknowledged. The story they were building rested on behavior, on words, and on what they argued was a man who knew too much.

## The Breakthrough

The literal breakthrough came from the ground. In July 2019, workers installing a pipeline in a rural stretch of Weld County, southeast of Greeley, uncovered human remains. Investigators identified them as Jonelle's. An autopsy determined she had died of a gunshot wound to the head, and the Weld County Sheriff's Office began treating the matter as a homicide. After thirty-four years, the central, terrible question of whether she was alive was answered.

The discovery accelerated everything. In October 2020, a Weld County grand jury indicted Steve Pankey. The first trial opened in the fall of 2021 and ended in a mistrial: jurors could not reach a unanimous verdict on the murder and kidnapping counts, though they did convict him of false reporting to authorities. Prosecutors, led by Weld County District Attorney Michael Rourke, chose to try the case again.

The retrial reached its verdict on October 31, 2022. The jury found Pankey guilty of felony murder, second-degree kidnapping, and false reporting. It is important to be precise here: jurors acquitted him of first-degree premeditated murder. The conviction for felony murder, a killing committed in the course of another felony, carried the gravest consequence available under the law in place at the time of the crime. That same day, Judge Timothy Kerns sentenced Pankey to life in prison with the possibility of parole after twenty years. Pankey maintained his innocence to the end, telling the court, "I am a Christian. I will be in heaven. I am innocent and this is not justice for Jonelle."

## Where the Case Stands Today

The case is closed, and it is recorded as solved. Steve Pankey was convicted at retrial and is serving a life sentence in the Colorado prison system, eligible to seek parole only after two decades, which would place any such hearing decades in the future. He has continued to assert that he did not commit the crime, and his defense signaled an intention to challenge the conviction, a posture consistent with the appellate path open to defendants in serious cases. Readers should understand that an appeal, if pursued, tests the conduct of the trial rather than reopening the jury's factual findings, which stand unless a court rules otherwise.

For Jonelle's family, the resolution arrived in two parts separated by years: first the recovery of her remains in 2019, which ended the cruelest uncertainty, and then the verdict in 2022. District Attorney Rourke, in the aftermath, made a deliberate choice to center Jonelle rather than the man convicted of killing her, saying he intended to stop speaking the defendant's name. The court, in sentencing, spoke of finality more than closure, acknowledging that some losses do not close.

## Why This Case Still Matters

Jonelle Matthews's case endures for reasons that reach beyond a single Colorado town. She became, in the 1980s, a national symbol of a fear that gripped the country, and her case helped fuel the institutions and habits, the hotlines, the alerts, the photographs, that grew up around missing children. That she was not found for nearly thirty-five years is a measure of how long grief can be made to wait.

It is also a case that asks hard questions about evidence and certainty. A jury convicted on a largely circumstantial record, in the acknowledged absence of physical proof linking the defendant to the child, and at the same time declined to find premeditation. That distinction is not a footnote. It reflects the careful, bounded work a jury is asked to do, weighing what can be proven against what cannot. The case stands as a reminder that justice, when it finally comes, often arrives incomplete, and that the most important name in any such story is not the one in the headlines but the child it began with.
