## Who Was Involved

At the center of this case are its two victims. The Reverend Edward Wheeler Hall was a forty-one-year-old Episcopal priest, rector of the Church of St. John the Evangelist in New Brunswick, New Jersey. By the accounts of the era he was a well-regarded clergyman, married into the prominent Stevens family and connected through that marriage to considerable inherited wealth. Eleanor Mills, thirty-four, sang in his church choir and was active in parish life. She was married to James Mills, the church sexton, and the couple lived in far more modest circumstances than the Halls.

Hall and Mills were, by widespread report among parishioners and neighbors at the time, romantically involved. That relationship, crossing lines of class, marriage, and the clergy, would become the lens through which the public read everything that followed.

The other figures who would come to define the case were never convicted of anything. Hall's widow, Frances Noel Stevens Hall, and her brothers Henry Stevens and William Stevens were eventually accused and brought to trial. They are entitled to the full presumption of innocence, which the courts ultimately affirmed.

## What Happened

On the morning of September 16, 1922, two young people walking along a quiet country lane near New Brunswick, an area known locally as a spot for trysting couples, came upon a man and a woman lying side by side beneath a crab apple tree. Both had been shot. They were the Reverend Edward Wheeler Hall and Eleanor Mills.

The scene drew immediate attention not only for the deaths themselves but for the details surrounding them. The bodies had been arranged, and scattered nearby were letters understood to have passed between the two. Within days the story of a married minister and a married choir singer, found dead together in a lovers' lane, had leapt from the local papers to the national press. It arrived at a moment when New York's competing tabloids were hungry for exactly this kind of story, and they seized it. As HISTORY notes, the New York tabloids "created a similar media sensation out of the 1922 murder of a reverend and choir singer in New Jersey," a precedent for the crime spectacles that followed throughout the decade.

## The Investigation

The investigation was troubled almost from the first hour. Jurisdiction was contested between Somerset and Middlesex counties, a confusion that cost time and coordination. Meanwhile, as contemporary accounts and later historians describe, curiosity-seekers descended on the site, trampling the ground, carrying off souvenirs, and handling items connected to the scene. Physical evidence that might have mattered was compromised before it could be properly examined.

A grand jury reviewed the matter in 1922 but returned no indictments. The case went quiet for nearly four years. Then, in 1926, renewed press attention, driven heavily by one of the era's most aggressive tabloids, and fresh claims circulating in the papers prompted state officials to reopen the matter. New Jersey's governor ordered another investigation, and this time it led to charges. Frances Hall and her brothers Henry and William Stevens were indicted.

## The Breakthrough

It is important to be precise here, because what 1926 produced was a sensational trial, not a solution.

The trial opened in November 1926 at the Somerset County Courthouse in Somerville and ran for roughly a month. It drew enormous national coverage, fed by the wealth and social standing of the defendants and by the lurid contours of the underlying story. The prosecution's most theatrical witness was Jane Gibson, a local woman who raised hogs near the murder site and whom the press dubbed "the Pig Woman." She claimed to have been near the lane on the night of the killings and to have seen and heard a struggle in the dark. Her account, however, shifted over time, and her credibility was sharply contested by the defense. By the trial she was gravely ill and testified from a hospital bed brought into the courtroom.

After hearing dozens of witnesses, the jury deliberated and acquitted Frances Hall and her brothers. As the Encyclopedia.com account of the trial records, the crime "has never been solved," and on the books of the Somerset County prosecutor the Hall-Mills case continues to be unsolved. The acquitted defendants later pursued libel actions against the tabloid press, which were settled out of court. No one was ever convicted of the killings, and the "breakthrough" the public anticipated never came.

## Where the Case Stands Today

More than a century later, the Hall-Mills case remains officially unsolved. The 1926 acquittals closed the only prosecution the case ever produced, and no charge has been sustained against anyone since. The defendants who stood trial were found not guilty, and that verdict stands.

What endures instead is documentation. Institutions such as the New Brunswick Free Public Library have preserved collections tied to the case, including court testimony, depositions, newspaper clippings, and digitized tabloid editions from the 1926 trial, alongside later research materials. These archives allow historians to revisit the evidence and the coverage, even as the central question of who killed Edward Hall and Eleanor Mills goes unanswered. The case has continued to draw serious scholarship, most recently Joe Pompeo's 2022 book "Blood & Ink," which examines both the crime and the press war it helped ignite.

## Why This Case Still Matters

The lasting significance of the Hall-Mills case lies less in any theory of the crime than in what it revealed about American media and public appetite. Reputable accounts consistently rank it among the most heavily reported trials in the nation's history, surpassed in its era only by the Lindbergh kidnapping case of the 1930s. It helped establish the template for the modern tabloid crime story: the intimate scandal, the wealthy family, the carnival at the crime scene, the star witness, the verdict that satisfies no one.

For an archive devoted to remembering victims with seriousness, the case is also a caution. Edward Hall and Eleanor Mills were real people whose deaths became entertainment within days. The frenzy that made their story famous also helped destroy the evidence and the sober process that might have answered for them. A hundred years on, the most honest thing that can be said is that two people were killed beneath a crab apple tree in New Jersey, that the law tried once and acquitted, and that the question of who was responsible remains open.
