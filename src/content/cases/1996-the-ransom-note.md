## Who Was Involved

JonBenét Ramsey was six years old. She lived with her parents, John and Patsy Ramsey, and her older brother, Burke, in a large home in Boulder, Colorado. John Ramsey was a successful technology executive; Patsy was a former pageant titleholder. The family had moved in Boulder's affluent, low-crime circles, the kind of community whose police, as later reporting noted, had little routine experience with homicide.

It is important to be precise about everyone named in this case, because for years the absence of an arrest invited speculation that fell hardest on the family. That speculation does not survive the evidence. In 2008 the Boulder District Attorney formally cleared John, Patsy, and Burke Ramsey based on DNA testing, a point this article returns to in detail. Patsy Ramsey did not live to see it: she died of ovarian cancer in 2006. No person has ever been charged in connection with JonBenét's death, and everyone discussed here retains the presumption of innocence.

The other central figure in this story is not a person at all. It is a document: a handwritten note, two and a half pages long, that turned an early-morning report of a missing child into a murder investigation that has lasted nearly three decades.

## What Happened

In the pre-dawn hours of December 26, 1996, Patsy Ramsey called 911 to report her daughter missing. She told police she had come downstairs and found a long handwritten note on a staircase inside the home. According to reporting by CBS News, the note ran two and a half pages and demanded a ransom of $118,000, with instructions warning against contacting authorities.

The Ramseys called police anyway. Officers arrived to find no signs of forced entry. The household filled with arriving friends and family while investigators initially treated the matter as a kidnapping. Hours later, after a detective asked John Ramsey and a family friend to look through the house, JonBenét's body was found in a basement room. She had died of strangulation and a head injury. The medical details are kept brief here out of respect for a child victim; the essential fact is that what began as a reported abduction was now a homicide, and the only ransom demand had come in the form of that note.

No one ever attempted to collect the $118,000.

## The Investigation

From the first morning, the investigation drew criticism that has never fully faded. Only JonBenét's bedroom was sealed off; the rest of the home was not secured, and visitors moved through rooms that would later be recognized as part of a crime scene. The discovery of the body by a family member, after officers had already been in the house, further compromised the scene.

Within the investigation, the ransom note became [the most argued-over piece of evidence](/the-evidence-room) in the case. Several features struck investigators as unusual. The note was extraordinarily long for a ransom demand; the FBI has noted it is highly atypical for such a note to be composed at the scene. It was written with a pen and notepad from inside the Ramsey home, and a practice draft was also found in the house. And the dollar figure, $118,000, closely matched a work bonus John Ramsey had received, a detail he himself pointed out to early responders.

[Handwriting analysis](/cases/btk-dennis-rader) added more questions than answers. Examiners with the Colorado Bureau of Investigation concluded John Ramsey did not write the note, and Burke was excluded as well. The findings as to Patsy Ramsey were described as inconclusive, meaning she was neither identified as the author nor definitively ruled out on handwriting alone. That ambiguity, unsupported by other physical evidence pointing to her, fueled years of public suspicion.

Suspicion reached a formal threshold in 1999. As ABC News reported when the records were unsealed in 2013, a Colorado grand jury had voted to indict John and Patsy Ramsey on counts of child abuse resulting in death and accessory to a crime. The indictment did not accuse either parent of the killing itself. Crucially, then-District Attorney Alex Hunter declined to sign the indictment or prosecute, concluding the evidence was insufficient to support charges. The case moved forward unsolved.

## The Breakthrough

In a case still officially unsolved, the closest thing to a breakthrough is forensic rather than an arrest, and it cut in the direction of exoneration.

Investigators had long known that a mixed blood sample from JonBenét's clothing contained DNA from an unidentified male. By 2003 that profile was strong enough to be entered into the FBI's national database, where it matched no one. In 2008, the Boulder District Attorney's Office, then led by Mary Lacy, applied a newer technique called "touch DNA," which recovers genetic material from skin cells left by handling. As CBS News reported, testing of the waistband areas of the long underwear JonBenét was wearing recovered male DNA that matched the unknown profile already found on her clothing.

Lacy concluded that the same unidentified male DNA, found in more than one place on the garments JonBenét was wearing, pointed to an outside perpetrator. On that basis, the District Attorney's Office formally cleared the family. In a letter to John Ramsey, Lacy wrote that the office did not consider him, Patsy, or Burke "to be under any suspicion in the commission of this crime," and added a direct apology: "To the extent that we may have contributed in any way to the public perception that you might have been involved in this crime, I am deeply sorry."

The 2008 exoneration was not universally embraced inside law enforcement, and some investigators continued to view the conclusion as broader than the science strictly required. But as a matter of public record, it remains the District Attorney's formal position, and it superseded the suspicion reflected in the 1999 grand jury vote.

## Where the Case Stands Today

The Boulder Police Department continues to treat the killing as an open, active homicide. On its official case page, the department notes it has processed more than 21,000 tips and interviewed over 1,000 people across many states, and it has pushed back on the claim that viable DNA leads are going unexamined, calling that assertion "completely false."

Recent work centers on DNA. As CBS News Colorado has reported, Boulder police, working with the Colorado Bureau of Investigation, have been testing newer items and retesting older evidence as forensic methods improve. The family, led by John Ramsey, has publicly pressed for forensic genetic genealogy, the database-and-family-tree approach that identified the Golden State Killer suspect, and has asked that items they believe were never fully processed be examined.

The obstacle is the nature of the sample itself. Forensic experts cited by CBS News Colorado have cautioned that the key biological evidence is a degraded, mixed profile, and that genealogy methods may not yet be sensitive enough to build a usable family tree from it. The science is advancing; whether it can resolve this particular sample remains, for now, an open question. No new, case-resolving DNA identification has been publicly confirmed.

## Why This Case Still Matters

The JonBenét Ramsey case endures in the public imagination partly for tragic reasons and partly for instructive ones. It is a near-textbook study in how a crime scene can be lost in the first hours, and how that loss can shape everything that follows. It is also a cautionary tale about the court of public opinion: a family was treated as guilty for more than a decade before being formally cleared, and one parent died still under that cloud.

The ransom note remains the case's central artifact precisely because it resolves so little. It is long where such notes are short, handwritten at the scene where such notes are prepared in advance, and built around a figure tied to the household. It points inward and outward at once, and almost thirty years on it still refuses to settle the question it raised on December 26, 1996. What the evidence does establish, clearly and on the record, is who did not do this: the Boulder District Attorney cleared the Ramsey family by DNA in 2008. The rest of the answer is still waiting on a name the science has not yet produced.
