## Who Was Involved

Asha Jaquilla Degree was nine years old in the winter of 2000, a child known and loved in Shelby, North Carolina, a small city in Cleveland County in the western part of the state. She lived with her parents, Harold and Iquilla Degree, and her older brother. By every account she was a careful, obedient girl, the kind of child who did not wander, did not break rules, and did not leave home in the dark. That is precisely what makes what happened to her so difficult to explain, and so impossible for her family and her community to set aside. To the people of Shelby she became, over the years, "Shelby's Sweetheart," a phrase that carries both affection and grief.

The people whose names now appear most often alongside Asha's are not loved ones but persons of interest, identified by investigators only recently. They include members of a Cleveland County family, the Dedmons, and a man named Russell Bradley Underhill, who died in 2004. None of these individuals has been charged with any crime, and each connection described by authorities remains an allegation that has not been tested in court.

## What Happened

Sometime in the predawn hours of February 14, 2000, Asha left her family's home. Her father reported last checking on the children at roughly 2:30 a.m., and her mother discovered her missing around 6:30 a.m. There was no sign of forced entry. For reasons that have never been established, Asha appears to have packed a bag and walked out into a cold, heavy rainstorm.

In the hours that followed, a small number of motorists reported seeing a child walking alone along North Carolina Highway 18, roughly a mile or so from the Degree home, around 4 a.m. According to later accounts from witnesses, when one driver slowed or attempted to approach, the child moved off the roadside, at one point reportedly running toward a wooded area. Those scattered sightings are the last credible traces of Asha. Despite extensive ground searches in the days afterward, she was not located. A nine-year-old girl had walked into the rain and, as far as anyone could prove, simply vanished.

## The Investigation

The Degree case became one of North Carolina's most enduring missing-child investigations, drawing in the Cleveland County Sheriff's Office, the North Carolina State Bureau of Investigation, and the FBI. For years the central, agonizing question was why a cautious child would leave her bed in the middle of the night, and the absence of an answer kept the case open and active long after many would have gone cold by default.

Investigators returned to the file repeatedly, re-interviewing witnesses and re-examining evidence. A reward was offered for information leading to an arrest and conviction, growing over the years; by the 25th and 26th anniversaries it had reached as much as $100,000. The FBI publicized the case on its major anniversaries and asked the public for help, releasing details about items connected to Asha in the hope that someone would recognize them. For more than two decades, the investigation pressed forward largely on the strength of a single recovered piece of physical evidence and the determination not to let the case fade.

## The Breakthrough

That piece of evidence surfaced in August 2001, about a year and a half after Asha disappeared. Workers on a construction project off Highway 18 in Burke County, near Morganton and roughly twenty-six to thirty miles north of Shelby, uncovered Asha's book bag. It had been wrapped in black plastic trash bags and buried. Inside were items that did not belong to her, including a New Kids on the Block concert T-shirt and a copy of Dr. Seuss's "McElligot's Pool," a book traced to the library at her elementary school but not one she had checked out. The FBI sent the materials to its laboratory for analysis. For years, the buried bag raised far more questions than it answered.

The forensic significance of that evidence became clear only much later. In September 2024, the FBI, the State Bureau of Investigation, and the Cleveland County Sheriff's Office executed a series of search warrants on properties in the county. Sheriff Alan Norman said the warrants were obtained "based on physical evidence directly connected to Asha's disappearance." It was around this time that authorities stated, for the first time, that they believed Asha had been killed, reframing a missing-child case as a suspected homicide. According to the search-warrant filings, DNA testing on items associated with the recovered bag and clothing linked to two persons of interest: Russell Bradley Underhill and a member of the Dedmon family. Investigators also towed a vintage green vehicle from a Dedmon property, noting it resembled the type of older green car they had long sought in connection with the case, though it was not an exact match to earlier descriptions.

Subsequent filings in early 2025 described additional material, including seized phones and a witness account alleging that one of the Dedmon daughters had, years earlier and while intoxicated, made a statement suggesting involvement in Asha's death. It is important to state plainly what these documents are: allegations contained in investigative filings and untested witness claims. The family named in the warrants has, through their attorney, firmly denied any connection to Asha's disappearance, calling the link "tenuous at best." In April 2025, investigators conducted further searches at new locations, including a former school whose owner consented to the search and was described by authorities as unconnected to the investigation. No human remains were recovered in any of these searches.

## Where the Case Stands Today

As of the most recent reporting in early 2026, the case remains unresolved. Authorities have publicly identified members of the Dedmon family as persons of interest or suspects, and have stated they believe Asha was the victim of a homicide. But no one has been arrested, and no one has been charged. Each of the named individuals is entitled to the presumption of innocence, and all have denied involvement. Asha Degree has never been found. The reward for information leading to a conviction stands at up to $100,000, and the Cleveland County Sheriff's Office and the State Bureau of Investigation continue to ask anyone with knowledge to come forward.

## Why This Case Still Matters

More than a quarter century has passed since a nine-year-old girl walked out of her home into the rain, and the central mystery, why she left and what became of her, is still without a proven answer. The case endures partly because of its strangeness and partly because of what it has demanded of those who refused to give up: the patient preservation of a buried book bag, the slow maturation of DNA science, the willingness to keep a file open across generations of investigators. For Asha's family, none of that has yet delivered the one thing they have asked for, which is to know what happened and to bring her home. Until that day comes, the most honest thing this archive can offer is to keep her name, and the facts of her disappearance, set down clearly and treated with care.
