## Who Was Involved

The people of Hinterkaifeck were three generations of a single rural household, plus a young woman who had worked for them for barely a day. The farm belonged to the family of Andreas Gruber, an elderly farmer of about 63, and his wife, Cäzilia, who was around 72. Living with them were their widowed daughter, Viktoria Gabriel, who was 35, and Viktoria's two children: seven-year-old Cäzilia and two-year-old Josef. The sixth person was Maria Baumgartner, a maid in her forties who had only just arrived to take up her position. According to records held in the Bavarian state archives, Baumgartner reached the farm on the afternoon of Friday, 31 March 1922, escorted by her sister, who stayed about an hour and then left. The sister was very likely the last outsider to see the household alive.

The Grubers kept to themselves. They worked a small holding at the end of a forest track, roughly 70 kilometres north of Munich, in Upper Bavaria. The family's life had not been free of hardship or scandal, and the household had been the subject of unkind talk in the surrounding villages for years. Those tensions would later complicate the search for the killer, but on the last evening anyone saw them, the people of Hinterkaifeck were simply a family settling in a new servant and preparing for the night.

## What Happened

In the days before the killings, the family reported a series of unsettling events that have since become inseparable from the story. The archival accounts, drawn from later police interviews, record that Andreas Gruber found footprints in the fresh snow leading from the edge of the woods toward the farm, with no tracks leading away again. Days earlier, the household had found a Munich newspaper on the property that no one could account for, since no one in the area subscribed to it. Family members described feeling watched from the nearby forest, and Gruber told several people he had heard someone moving in the attic at night, though a search turned up no one. He is recorded as having declined offers of help.

On the night of 31 March into 1 April 1922, all six people were killed. Investigators later reconstructed that four of them, the elder Grubers, Viktoria, and the little girl Cäzilia, were drawn or led one by one into the barn and killed there, while Josef in his cot and Maria Baumgartner in her room were killed inside the house. The weapon was a mattock, a heavy pickaxe-like farm tool, which appears to have belonged to the farm itself. Out of respect for the victims, the details of their injuries are not recounted here. What is documented is that the violence was deliberate and that the household was wiped out in a single night.

## The Investigation

The bodies were not discovered for roughly four days. The seven-year-old's absence from school and the family's failure to appear at Sunday worship drew some notice, but the Grubers were known to keep apart, so little was made of it at first. On 4 April a mechanic came to the property, spent hours there, and met no one. Word reached neighbours, who went to the farm and found the dead.

By the time investigators from the Munich police, led by Inspector Georg Reingruber, reached the isolated farm, the scene had been badly compromised. News had spread and curious onlookers had already come and gone, very probably destroying important traces; a tracking dog could pick up no usable scent. Several details deepened the mystery rather than resolving it. The animals had been fed and tended in the days after the killings, smoke had been seen rising from the chimney, and food in the house appeared to have been used, all of which suggested to investigators that someone, presumably the killer, had remained at Hinterkaifeck after the murders. Robbery seemed an unlikely motive, because substantial money and valuables were later found on the property.

## The Breakthrough

There was, in the end, no breakthrough. This is the hard truth at the centre of the Hinterkaifeck case. The original inquiry under Inspector Reingruber continued through the 1920s without producing a definitive lead, and over the following decades more than a hundred people were questioned as possible suspects. A neighbour who had a complicated history with the family became one of the most persistent names attached to the case, but he was never charged and successfully sued people who publicly called him the murderer before his death in 1941. No suspect was ever convicted.

Time worked steadily against any solution. The farmhouse was demolished in 1923, and although the mattock believed to be the weapon was recovered around that time, key physical evidence was later lost; archival accounts note that material connected to the case was destroyed when Augsburg was bombed in 1944. Modern reviews have not closed the gap. In 2007 students at a Bavarian police academy re-examined the case with contemporary methods and concluded that it could no longer be definitively solved, given the degraded evidence and the deaths of those involved. They reportedly settled on a theory but chose not to name a suspect, out of respect for surviving relatives. The case has never been officially solved.

## Where the Case Stands Today

More than a century on, Hinterkaifeck remains one of Germany's most famous unsolved crimes, described by the Historisches Lexikon Bayerns as among the most spectacular and best-known criminal cases in Bavaria. The farmstead itself is gone, demolished the year after the killings, and a memorial now marks the place where it once stood. The victims were buried in a nearby cemetery.

What survives is the documentary record, held in Bavarian state and Munich police archives, and an enormous body of later retelling. Much of what circulates about the case online is unreliable, mixing documented facts with rumour and invention, so caution is warranted with any vivid claim that cannot be traced to a credible source. The verifiable core is stark enough: six people killed in one night, a scene compromised before it could be properly examined, and a perpetrator who was never identified.

## Why This Case Still Matters

Hinterkaifeck endures partly because of its eerie particulars, the footprints with no return, the sounds in the attic, the sense that the killer may have lived calmly among the dead, but it deserves to be remembered for more than its capacity to unsettle. At its heart is a family, including two small children and a young woman starting a new job, whose lives were ended and whose killer was never brought to account.

The case is also a study in how investigations fail. The scene was overrun before it could be secured, leads were pursued late or not at all, and the social taboos of the era made some lines of inquiry difficult to follow openly. Those failures, as much as the crime itself, shaped the century of uncertainty that followed. For a serious true-crime archive, Hinterkaifeck is a reminder that not every story resolves, that careful handling of evidence matters enormously, and that the people behind a famous mystery were not characters in a legend but a household that vanished from a quiet Bavarian farm one night in the spring of 1922.
